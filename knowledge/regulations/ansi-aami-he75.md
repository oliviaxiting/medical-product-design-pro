# Overview

## Purpose

ANSI/AAMI HE75 is a human factors engineering guideline developed for the design of medical devices and healthcare systems.

Its primary objective is to improve safety, effectiveness, efficiency, and usability by ensuring that products are designed around human capabilities and limitations rather than technical implementation.

Unlike regulatory standards that define compliance requirements, HE75 provides evidence-based design guidance that supports the creation of interfaces, workflows, controls, displays, alarms, and physical interactions that reduce use errors and improve clinical performance.

For AI-assisted product design, HE75 should be treated as a foundational human factors knowledge source rather than a checklist.

The principles within HE75 should influence every stage of product design, including:

- Product Definition
- Requirement Analysis
- Information Architecture
- Workflow Design
- Interaction Design
- Visual Design
- Design Systems
- Usability Evaluation
- Design Review

---

## Scope

The knowledge in this document applies to:

- Medical Device Software
- Clinical Information Systems
- Patient Monitoring Systems
- Imaging Systems
- Medical Workstations
- Bedside Devices
- Mobile Medical Applications
- Tablet Applications
- Clinical Dashboards
- Embedded Medical Interfaces

The guidance is intended for multidisciplinary teams including product managers, UX designers, UI designers, engineers, clinical specialists, usability engineers, and regulatory professionals.

## Design Objective

The objective of applying HE75 is not to create visually attractive interfaces.

The objective is to enable users to complete clinical tasks safely, accurately, efficiently, and with minimal cognitive effort.

Good interface design reduces the probability of use error while improving user confidence and decision quality.

---

## Core Philosophy

Human-centered design begins with understanding people rather than interfaces.

Medical interfaces should adapt to human perception, cognition, memory, attention, and physical capabilities.

Design decisions should always prioritize:

- Patient Safety
- Clinical Effectiveness
- Workflow Efficiency
- Cognitive Simplicity
- Error Prevention
- Learnability
- Consistency
- Maintainability

Visual appearance should support these goals rather than replace them.

# Human Factors Principles

## Principle

Human factors engineering recognizes that most use errors result from mismatches between system design and human capabilities, rather than user incompetence.

Effective medical products are designed to support how people naturally perceive, think, decide, and act.

Interfaces should adapt to users rather than expecting users to adapt to interfaces.

---

## Why

Clinical users often work under conditions that include:

- High patient volume
- Multiple simultaneous tasks
- Frequent interruptions
- Time-critical decisions
- Emotional stress
- Limited attention
- Environmental noise

Under these conditions, users are more likely to make errors if interfaces require excessive memory, complex navigation, or unnecessary interpretation.

Design should reduce the mental effort required to perform safe and effective clinical work.

---

## Guideline

Design should respect the following human capabilities and limitations.

### Perception

Ensure that important information can be detected quickly.

Provide sufficient visual contrast.

Avoid visual clutter.

Separate unrelated information.

Group related information together.

---

### Attention

Users cannot attend to every element simultaneously.

Highlight only truly important information.

Avoid unnecessary visual emphasis.

Reduce competing visual signals.

---

### Memory

Avoid requiring users to remember previous values, hidden information, or complex procedures.

Whenever possible:

Display information instead of expecting recall.

Provide contextual guidance.

Keep task history visible when necessary.

---

### Decision Making

Present information according to clinical priority.

Support rapid comparison.

Reduce ambiguity.

Clearly communicate system status.

Provide meaningful feedback after user actions.

---

### Motor Performance

Interactive elements should be easy to locate and activate.

Frequently used controls should remain within predictable locations.

Avoid unnecessary pointer movement.

Minimize repetitive actions.

Support efficient task completion.

---

## AI Rules

Always reduce cognitive workload before improving visual appearance.

Always present information according to clinical importance.

Always minimize memory requirements.

Always provide immediate feedback after user actions.

Always make critical information immediately recognizable.

Prefer recognition over recall.

Prefer simplicity over feature density.

Prefer predictable interactions over novel interactions.

Never assume experienced users can compensate for poor design.

Never hide safety-critical information.

Never require multiple navigation steps to reach frequently used functions.

Never separate strongly related information across multiple screens without clear justification.

- System feedback is timely and understandable.

# Interface Design Guidelines

## Principle

Medical interface design should enable clinicians to complete tasks safely, efficiently, and with confidence. Interface structure should be driven by clinical workflow rather than technical implementation or visual preference.

A well-designed interface minimizes cognitive effort, supports rapid decision-making, and reduces the likelihood of use errors.

---

## Why

Clinical environments are dynamic and demanding. Users often work under time pressure, switch frequently between tasks, and manage multiple patients or devices simultaneously.

Poor interface organization forces users to search, remember, and interpret information unnecessarily, increasing workload and the probability of error.

Effective interface design should help users immediately understand:

- Where they are.
- What requires attention.
- What actions are available.
- What should happen next.

---

## Guideline

### Layout

Organize the interface according to clinical workflow.

Frequently used functions should remain in predictable locations.

Keep navigation stable across all pages.

Separate operational areas from information display areas.

---

### Visual Hierarchy

Clearly distinguish:

- Critical Information
- Operational Information
- Supporting Information

Visual hierarchy should communicate importance before users begin reading.

---

### Functional Grouping

Group controls and information according to clinical tasks rather than technical modules.

Functions that are frequently used together should be physically close.

Avoid splitting related tasks across multiple pages unless necessary.

---

### Navigation

Navigation should always answer:

- Where am I?
- Where can I go?
- How do I return?

Reduce navigation depth whenever possible.

---

## Console Application

Console interfaces primarily support continuous monitoring and operational management.

Design recommendations:

- Support high information density without sacrificing readability.
- Use multi-column layouts for simultaneous observation.
- Keep navigation persistently visible.
- Allow multiple information panels on one screen.
- Optimize for keyboard and mouse interaction.
- Support rapid switching between patients, devices, and workflows.
- Keep frequently used actions within immediate reach.

The Console should prioritize monitoring efficiency and operational continuity.

---

## PAD Application

PAD interfaces primarily support bedside interaction and focused clinical tasks.

Design recommendations:

- Reduce information density.
- Prioritize one primary task per screen.
- Use larger touch targets.
- Prefer card-based information grouping.
- Reduce navigation levels.
- Keep critical information immediately visible.
- Support operation while standing or moving.

The PAD should prioritize simplicity, touch usability, and rapid task completion.

---

## AI Rules

Always organize interfaces around clinical workflow.

Always group information according to user tasks.

Always prioritize patient safety over visual appearance.

For Console interfaces:

- Prefer monitoring efficiency.
- Support simultaneous information visibility.
- Optimize workspace utilization.

For PAD interfaces:

- Prefer interaction simplicity.
- Reduce touch operations.
- Keep the primary workflow on a single screen whenever possible.

Never design layouts according to database structure.

Never separate strongly related information.

Never increase interface complexity without measurable clinical benefit.

---

## Design Review Checklist

Before approving an interface, verify:

- Workflow is immediately understandable.
- Navigation is predictable.
- Critical information is prominent.
- Frequently used actions are easy to access.
- Information grouping supports clinical tasks.
- Console layouts support efficient monitoring.
- PAD layouts support efficient touch interaction.
- Users can identify the next action without hesitation.

# Information Design

## Principle

Information should be organized according to clinical importance, decision sequence, and user goals rather than data availability.

Users should spend their attention making clinical decisions—not searching for information.

Good information design improves situational awareness while reducing cognitive workload.

---

## Why

Medical systems often contain large volumes of clinical data.

Displaying all information with equal emphasis makes critical information harder to identify.

Information should guide the user's attention toward what is most important at the current moment.

Users should immediately recognize:

- What is happening.
- What requires action.
- What can wait.
- What information supports the current decision.

---

## Guideline

### Information Priority

Organize information into four levels:

- Critical
- Important
- Supporting
- Background

Display priority should always match clinical priority.

---

### Information Hierarchy

Use hierarchy through:

- Position
- Size
- Contrast
- Typography
- Spacing

Hierarchy should remain consistent throughout the product.

---

### Information Grouping

Group information according to:

- Clinical workflow
- Decision sequence
- User task

Avoid grouping information by backend modules or database structure.

---

### Status Communication

Clearly distinguish system status:

- Normal
- Warning
- Critical
- Offline
- Unknown

Critical status must never rely solely on color.

Always provide text, icons, or additional visual cues.

---

### Data Comparison

When comparison is required:

- Align related values.
- Maintain consistent units.
- Display trends clearly.
- Highlight clinically meaningful changes.

Never force users to compare values across different pages.

---

## Console Application

Console interfaces often require simultaneous observation of multiple patients, devices, or datasets.

Design recommendations:

- Display more information within one view.
- Support side-by-side comparison.
- Keep alarms, trends, and patient status continuously visible.
- Allow persistent secondary information.
- Optimize dashboard layouts for rapid monitoring.

Console interfaces should maximize situational awareness while avoiding visual overload.

---

## PAD Application

PAD interfaces should present only the information necessary for the current clinical task.

Design recommendations:

- Focus on one patient.
- Reduce competing information.
- Display only key clinical indicators by default.
- Use progressive disclosure for detailed information.
- Keep important actions close to relevant information.
- Reduce unnecessary scrolling.

PAD interfaces should support rapid bedside understanding.

---

## AI Rules

Always present information according to clinical priority.

Always minimize visual search.

Always support rapid comparison.

Always distinguish critical information from supporting information.

For Console interfaces:

- Support continuous monitoring.
- Maximize information efficiency.
- Maintain visibility of secondary information without reducing readability.

For PAD interfaces:

- Reduce information density.
- Prioritize rapid recognition.
- Display only information necessary for the current workflow.

Never overload users with equally emphasized information.

Never hide critical information within secondary pages.

Never require users to remember values that can be displayed.

---

## Design Review Checklist

Before approving information design, verify:

- Critical information is immediately visible.
- Information hierarchy reflects clinical priority.
- Related information is grouped logically.
- Comparison tasks are efficient.
- Status communication is unambiguous.
- Console layouts support continuous monitoring.
- PAD layouts support focused bedside decision-making.
- Information density matches the intended usage environment.

# Interaction Design

## Principle

Interaction design defines how users communicate with the system to accomplish clinical tasks safely, efficiently, and predictably.

Every interaction should reduce cognitive effort, minimize unnecessary operations, and provide immediate, understandable feedback.

Interactions should support clinical workflow rather than interrupt it.

---

## Why

Clinical users perform hundreds of interactions during a shift.

Small inefficiencies become significant over time and increase workload, fatigue, and the likelihood of use errors.

Effective interaction design enables users to:

- Complete tasks with minimal effort.
- Understand system responses immediately.
- Recover easily from mistakes.
- Maintain focus on patient care rather than software operation.

Good interaction design improves confidence, efficiency, and safety.

---

## Guideline

### Workflow First

Design interactions according to clinical workflow.

Each interaction should move the user naturally toward task completion.

Avoid unnecessary intermediate steps.

---

### Predictability

Users should always know:

- What will happen before an action.
- What happened after an action.
- What to do next.

Avoid unexpected behavior.

---

### Feedback

Every user action should receive timely feedback.

Feedback should indicate:

- Success
- Failure
- Processing
- Warning
- Completion

Feedback should be immediate, visible, and understandable.

---

### Error Recovery

Users should be able to recover safely from mistakes.

Whenever possible:

- Confirm irreversible actions.
- Support Undo for low-risk operations.
- Clearly explain errors.
- Provide recovery guidance.

Never leave users uncertain about system status.

---

### Efficiency

Reduce unnecessary:

- Clicks
- Touches
- Dialogs
- Navigation
- Data entry

High-frequency tasks should require the minimum number of interactions.

---

## Typical Design Scenarios

### Patient Detail

Interaction should support rapid review of patient information.

Frequently used actions should remain visible.

Navigation should not interrupt clinical thinking.

---

### Alarm Center

Interactions should prioritize alarm acknowledgement, investigation, and resolution.

Alarm handling should require as few steps as possible.

---

### Trend Review

Trend exploration should remain smooth.

Zooming, filtering, and time selection should be intuitive and reversible.

---

### Device Configuration

Configuration workflows should prevent accidental changes.

Critical settings should require explicit confirmation.

---

## Console Application

Console interactions support long-duration operation.

Design recommendations:

- Optimize for keyboard and mouse.
- Support shortcuts for repetitive tasks.
- Reduce cursor travel distance.
- Allow multi-window or multi-panel workflows.
- Support simultaneous monitoring while interacting.

Console should maximize operational efficiency.

---

## PAD Application

PAD interactions support bedside clinical work.

Design recommendations:

- Optimize for touch.
- Increase touch target size.
- Minimize typing.
- Prefer direct manipulation.
- Keep interactions within one hand whenever practical.
- Reduce navigation depth.

PAD should maximize interaction simplicity.

---

## AI Rules

Always design interactions around workflow.

Always provide immediate feedback.

Always reduce repetitive operations.

Always support safe error recovery.

For Console:

- Prefer operational efficiency.
- Support continuous monitoring.

For PAD:

- Prefer touch efficiency.
- Reduce interaction complexity.

Never interrupt clinical workflow without necessity.

Never require unnecessary confirmation dialogs.

Never hide system status after user actions.

---

## Design Review Checklist

Before approving interaction design, verify:

- Workflow remains uninterrupted.
- Feedback is immediate.
- Errors can be recovered safely.
- High-frequency tasks are optimized.
- Console interaction supports efficient operation.
- PAD interaction supports comfortable touch operation.

# Controls & Input

## Principle

Controls are the primary interface between users and the medical system.

Every control should communicate its purpose clearly, behave consistently, and minimize the possibility of incorrect operation.

Input methods should support efficient task completion while reducing physical and cognitive effort.

---

## Why

Poorly designed controls increase operation time, user uncertainty, and use errors.

Medical users should never hesitate because they cannot identify:

- What a control does.
- Whether it is available.
- Whether an action succeeded.
- Whether an operation is safe.

Controls should communicate these answers without additional interpretation.

---

## Guideline

### Control Visibility

Interactive controls should be immediately recognizable.

Primary actions should receive stronger visual emphasis than secondary actions.

Dangerous actions should be clearly distinguishable.

---

### Consistency

Maintain consistent:

- Button behavior
- Input methods
- Icons
- Terminology
- Keyboard behavior
- Touch behavior

Consistency reduces learning effort.

---

### Input Design

Minimize manual data entry.

Whenever possible:

- Select instead of type.
- Scan instead of enter.
- Remember previous values.
- Auto-fill known information.

Reduce unnecessary keyboard usage.

---

### Confirmation

Require confirmation only for:

- Irreversible actions.
- High-risk operations.
- Safety-critical changes.

Avoid excessive confirmation dialogs for routine work.

---

### Disabled Controls

Clearly explain why a control is unavailable.

Avoid leaving disabled controls without context.

---

## Typical Design Scenarios

Require explicit confirmation before final submission.

Clearly distinguish review from execution.

---

### Alarm Acknowledgement

Acknowledgement should require intentional interaction but remain efficient.

Avoid unnecessary confirmation dialogs.

---

### Patient Search

Support rapid searching with minimal typing.

Remember recent selections whenever appropriate.

---

### Device Configuration

Display current values before allowing changes.

Provide clear confirmation before applying critical settings.

---

## Console Application

Console controls support continuous professional operation.

Design recommendations:

- Support keyboard shortcuts.
- Optimize mouse efficiency.
- Reduce pointer movement.
- Keep frequently used controls persistently visible.
- Support batch operations where appropriate.

## PAD Application

PAD controls support direct touch interaction.

Design recommendations:

- Increase touch target size.
- Maintain adequate spacing.
- Reduce typing.
- Prefer direct selection.
- Keep controls within comfortable thumb reach.
- Prevent accidental touches.

## AI Rules

Always minimize manual input.

Always distinguish dangerous actions.

Always maintain control consistency.

For Console:

# Alerts & Alarms

## Principle

Alerts and alarms exist to communicate abnormal conditions that require user awareness or intervention.

Their purpose is not to attract attention indiscriminately, but to support timely, accurate, and appropriate clinical responses while minimizing alarm fatigue.

Alarm presentation should always match the clinical significance of the event.

---

## Why

Clinical users are exposed to hundreds of alarms during a typical shift.

Excessive, poorly prioritized, or repetitive alarms reduce trust in the system and increase the likelihood that important alarms will be ignored.

Effective alarm design enables clinicians to quickly determine:

- What happened.
- How serious the situation is.
- Which patient or device is affected.
- What action is required.
- Whether immediate intervention is necessary.

Alarm systems should improve situational awareness rather than increase stress.

---

## Guideline

### Alarm Priority

Every alarm should belong to a clearly defined priority level.

Suggested categories:

- Critical
- High
- Medium
- Low
- Informational

Alarm priority should determine its visual prominence, notification behavior, and expected response time.

---

### Alarm Visibility

Critical alarms should remain continuously visible until appropriately acknowledged or resolved.

Alarm status should never disappear automatically before user awareness is confirmed.

---

### Alarm Information

Each alarm should clearly communicate:

- Alarm title
- Patient or device
- Time
- Current status
- Severity
- Recommended action

Avoid vague or technical messages that require interpretation.

---

### Alarm Acknowledgement

Acknowledgement confirms that the user has seen the alarm.

Acknowledgement should never be confused with resolution.

Display acknowledgement status clearly.

---

### Alarm Resolution

Support users in understanding:

- Whether the condition still exists.
- Whether intervention succeeded.
- Whether additional action is required.

---

### Alarm Fatigue Prevention

Reduce unnecessary alarms by:

- Eliminating duplicate notifications.
- Suppressing repeated low-value alarms.
- Prioritizing actionable events.
- Preventing excessive visual competition.

Every alarm should have clinical value.

---

## Typical Design Scenarios

### Patient Monitoring

Display patient alarms continuously with severity clearly visible.

---

### Device Failure

Provide immediate indication of affected device, impact, and recommended action.

---

### Network Interruption

Clearly distinguish communication failure from patient physiological alarms.

---

### Alarm History

Allow users to review previous alarms without interrupting active monitoring.

---

## Console Application

Console interfaces often supervise multiple patients and devices simultaneously.

Design recommendations:

- Maintain a persistent alarm panel.
- Support alarm sorting and filtering.
- Enable rapid navigation from alarm to patient.
- Keep active alarms visible regardless of current page.
- Support efficient acknowledgement workflows.

---

## PAD Application

PAD interfaces typically support bedside response.

Design recommendations:

- Display only alarms relevant to the current patient or device.
- Reduce unnecessary alarm history.
- Use large touch targets for acknowledgement.
- Keep recommended actions immediately visible.

---

## AI Rules

Always prioritize alarms according to clinical severity.

Always distinguish acknowledgement from resolution.

Always communicate alarm context.

Always recommend actionable information.

For Console:

- Support simultaneous alarm monitoring.

For PAD:

- Support rapid bedside response.

Never rely solely on color.

Never hide active critical alarms.

Never generate alarms without clear clinical meaning.

---

## Design Review Checklist

Before approving alarm design, verify:

- Alarm priority is clear.
- Critical alarms remain visible.

# Accessibility

## Principle

Accessibility ensures that medical systems remain usable, readable, and safe for all intended users under all expected clinical conditions.

Accessibility is not only for users with disabilities.

In healthcare, accessibility also includes usability under stress, fatigue, poor lighting, personal protective equipment (PPE), gloves, and time-critical situations.

Accessible design improves patient safety, operational efficiency, and clinical confidence.

---

## Why

Medical professionals frequently work in environments with:

- Long working hours
- Fatigue
- Emergency situations
- Low ambient lighting
- Bright operating lights
- Protective gloves
- Masks and face shields
- Frequent interruptions

Interfaces should remain easy to perceive, understand, and operate under these conditions.

Poor accessibility increases cognitive effort and the likelihood of use errors.

---

## Guideline

### Readability

Ensure that all text remains legible.

Maintain sufficient:

- Font size
- Line spacing
- Contrast
- White space

Avoid long paragraphs.

Prefer short, meaningful labels.

---

### Color Usage

Color should enhance understanding rather than carry information alone.

Critical status should always combine:

- Color
- Icon
- Text
- Position

Avoid relying solely on red or green.

---

### Touch Accessibility

Touch targets should support reliable operation.

Maintain adequate spacing.

Prevent accidental activation.

Support operation while wearing gloves whenever applicable.

---

### Keyboard & Mouse Accessibility

Console interfaces should support:

- Keyboard navigation
- Shortcut keys
- Visible focus indicators
- Predictable tab order

Frequently used operations should not depend solely on mouse interaction.

---

### Visual Accessibility

Avoid excessive visual clutter.

Limit simultaneous visual emphasis.

Ensure sufficient contrast between foreground and background.

---

## Typical Design Scenarios

### ICU Monitoring

Critical values should remain readable from a distance.

---

### Bedside PAD

Controls should remain operable while wearing gloves.

---

### Clinical Dashboard

Large information areas should remain readable during prolonged monitoring.

---

## Console Application

Design recommendations:

- Support efficient keyboard operation.
- Provide scalable layouts for different monitor sizes.
- Maintain readability on high-density dashboards.
- Keep important controls permanently visible.

---

## PAD Application

Design recommendations:

- Increase touch target size.
- Reduce precision requirements.
- Support landscape orientation.
- Maintain readability while standing beside the patient.
- Reduce scrolling whenever possible.

---

## AI Rules

Always prioritize readability.

Always maintain sufficient contrast.

Always combine color with icons and text.

Always support operation under clinical conditions.

For Console:

- Optimize keyboard efficiency.

For PAD:

- Optimize touch accuracy.

Never communicate safety-critical information using color alone.

Never reduce readability for visual aesthetics.

---

## Design Review Checklist

Before approving accessibility design, verify:

- Text remains readable.
- Color is not the only communication method.
- Touch targets are sufficiently large.
- Keyboard operation is supported where appropriate.
- Important information remains visible.
- Console supports prolonged operation.
- PAD supports reliable bedside interaction.

# Environmental Considerations

## Principle

Medical products should be designed for the environments in which they are actually used rather than ideal laboratory conditions.

Environmental factors directly influence perception, decision-making, interaction accuracy, and overall safety.

Design should remain effective despite physical and cognitive challenges.

---

## Why

Clinical environments are unpredictable.

Users may experience:

- Noise
- Poor lighting
- Bright lighting
- Frequent interruptions
- Time pressure
- Multiple simultaneous patients
- Physical movement
- Emergency situations

Interfaces should continue to support safe operation without requiring additional effort.

---

## Guideline

### Lighting Conditions

Maintain adequate contrast under:

- Bright clinical lighting
- Dim night environments
- Mixed lighting conditions

Avoid visual elements that disappear under different lighting conditions.

---

### Noise

Do not depend exclusively on sound.

Critical events should always include visual feedback.

Support operation even when audio alarms cannot be heard.

---

### Interruptions

Users may leave a task and return later.

Preserve context whenever possible.

Avoid forcing users to restart workflows.

---

### Time Pressure

High-frequency tasks should require minimal interaction.

Reduce unnecessary confirmations.

Support rapid recognition.

---

### Shared Workstations

Support quick user switching.

Protect patient privacy.

Avoid leaving confidential information visible after logout.

---

### Long-duration Monitoring

Console users may monitor systems continuously for several hours.

Reduce visual fatigue.

Maintain consistent layouts.

Avoid unnecessary animation.

---

## Typical Design Scenarios

### ICU Central Station

Support monitoring of multiple patients under continuous observation.

---

### Bedside PAD

Support rapid patient review while moving between beds.

---

### Device Configuration

Support configuration under maintenance and emergency conditions.

---

## Console Application

Design recommendations:

- Optimize for prolonged monitoring.
- Reduce eye movement.
- Keep alarms visible.
- Support multiple displays where appropriate.
- Maintain layout consistency across sessions.

---

## PAD Application

Design recommendations:

- Optimize for mobility.
- Reduce interaction depth.
- Support quick patient identification.
- Keep essential functions available within one or two interactions.

---

## AI Rules

Always consider the intended clinical environment.

Always minimize interaction under time pressure.

Always preserve workflow continuity.

Always support interrupted tasks.

For Console:

- Optimize prolonged monitoring.

For PAD:

- Optimize rapid bedside interaction.

Never assume ideal environmental conditions.

Never require unnecessary workflow repetition.

---

## Design Review Checklist

Before approving environmental adaptation, verify:

- Lighting conditions have been considered.
- Noise does not reduce safety.
- Interrupted workflows can be resumed.
- Time-critical tasks are efficient.
- Console supports prolonged monitoring.
- PAD supports mobile bedside use.
- Environmental factors do not compromise usability.
