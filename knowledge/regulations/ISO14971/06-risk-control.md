# Risk Control

## Purpose

This document defines how risks should be reduced according to ISO 14971.

The objective is to implement effective measures that reduce risk to an acceptable level while preserving clinical effectiveness.

Risk control should prioritize design improvements over procedural controls.

---

## Human Factors Principle

The most effective risk control is preventing hazardous situations from occurring.

Whenever possible, eliminate hazards through design rather than relying on user behavior.

---

## Risk Control Hierarchy

Apply controls in the following order:

### 1. Inherently Safe Design

Eliminate or reduce the hazard through interface or system design.

Examples:

- Simplify workflows
- Prevent invalid actions
- Improve visibility
- Reduce ambiguity

---

### 2. Protective Measures

Introduce safeguards that reduce risk when hazards cannot be eliminated.

Examples:

- Confirmation dialogs
- Warnings
- Visual emphasis
- Input validation
- Access control

---

### 3. Information for Safety

Provide guidance when residual risks remain.

Examples:

- User instructions
- Training
- Help documentation
- Safety labels

Training should not replace good interface design.

---

## Console Application

Examples include:

- Persistent alarm visibility
- Multi-patient verification
- Trend comparison
- Configuration review

---

## PAD Application

Examples include:

- Larger touch targets
- Simplified workflows
- Clear patient identification
- Immediate feedback

---

## Verification

After implementing controls, verify that:

- The risk has been reduced.
- No new hazards have been introduced.
- Clinical workflow remains efficient.

---

## AI Thinking Rules

For every unacceptable risk:

Determine whether it can be:

- Eliminated
- Prevented
- Detected
- Mitigated

Always recommend the highest-level control that is practical.

Avoid relying solely on warnings or training.
