# Hazard Identification

## Purpose

This document defines how hazards should be identified according to ISO 14971.

The objective is to recognize conditions that could potentially cause harm before they occur.

Hazard identification should begin during product definition and continue throughout development.

---

## Human Factors Principle

A hazard is a potential source of harm.

Hazards may originate from:

- User interaction
- Interface design
- Software behavior
- Hardware failure
- Environmental conditions
- Clinical workflow

Interfaces themselves may become hazards if they contribute to incorrect clinical decisions.

---

## Hazard Categories

Typical hazard categories include:

### Clinical Hazards

Examples:

- Delayed treatment
- Incorrect therapy
- Missed diagnosis

---

### Interface Hazards

Examples:

- Hidden alarms
- Ambiguous terminology
- Poor visibility
- Confusing navigation

---

### Operational Hazards

Examples:

- Wrong patient selection
- Incorrect parameter entry
- Device misconfiguration

---

### Environmental Hazards

Examples:

- Noise
- Low lighting
- Interruptions
- PPE
- Emergency situations

---

## Console Application

Common hazards include:

- Alarm overload
- Information overload
- Monitoring interruption
- Misreading trends

---

## PAD Application

Common hazards include:

- Touch errors
- Small touch targets
- Wrong patient confirmation
- Bedside interruptions

---

## AI Thinking Rules

For every interface, AI should identify:

What could go wrong?

Why could it happen?

Could this become a clinical hazard?

Could the interface prevent it?

Hazards should be identified before interface implementation.
