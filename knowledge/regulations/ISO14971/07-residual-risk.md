# Residual Risk

## Purpose

This document defines how residual risks should be evaluated according to ISO 14971.

The objective is to determine whether remaining risks are acceptable after all practical risk control measures have been implemented.

Residual risk should always be reviewed before product release.

---

## Human Factors Principle

No medical device is completely risk-free.

After appropriate risk controls have been applied, some level of residual risk may remain.

The objective is to ensure that remaining risks are understood, documented, and judged to be acceptable.

---

## Residual Risk Process

The evaluation process includes:

Identified Hazard

↓

Risk Analysis

↓

Risk Control

↓

Verification

↓

Residual Risk Evaluation

↓

Release Decision

Residual risk should be assessed after verifying that risk controls are effective.

---

## Evaluation Criteria

Residual risks should be reviewed considering:

- Remaining severity
- Remaining probability
- Clinical workflow
- User capability
- Patient safety
- Intended medical benefit

Residual risks should not exceed predefined acceptability criteria.

---

## Documentation

For each residual risk, document:

- Hazard
- Applied controls
- Remaining risk
- Justification for acceptability
- Additional recommendations if required

Documentation should support traceability throughout the product lifecycle.

---

## Console Application

Typical residual risks include:

- Alarm fatigue
- Information overload
- Delayed response
- Workflow interruption

Evaluate whether remaining risks are acceptable during routine clinical operation.

---

## PAD Application

Typical residual risks include:

- Touch errors
- Bedside interruptions
- Incorrect patient confirmation
- Accidental activation

Evaluate whether the interface continues to support safe bedside use after controls are applied.

---

## AI Thinking Rules

After proposing a design, AI should ask:

What risks still remain?

Can any remaining risks be further reduced?

Are users adequately informed about unavoidable risks?

Is the remaining risk acceptable for the intended clinical use?

Never assume that implementing controls eliminates all risk.
